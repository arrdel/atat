Abstract

Discrete diffusion language models (DLMs) enable parallel generation and bidirectional context but underperform autoregressive (AR) models due to uniform token treatment during masking. We introduce ATAT (Adaptive Token Attention for Text), which learns token-level importance through a hybrid formulation combining contextual signals from a frozen GPT-2 anchor with frequency-based priors:

$$i^l = 0.7 \cdot i^l_{\text{context}} + 0.3 \cdot i^l_{\text{freq}}$$

This hybrid achieves an 8.4% relative perplexity reduction over uniform masking (39.03 vs 42.31), demonstrating genuine signal complementarity. More importantly, ATAT matches ADLM's generation quality using 3.5× fewer trainable parameters (49M vs 170M) and 10× fewer training tokens (6.5B vs 65B) — establishing a new efficiency frontier for discrete diffusion LMs. We introduce a balanced masking curriculum that preserves important tokens early (semantic anchoring) and focuses on them late (capacity allocation). Theoretically, we prove importance-guided masking reduces sample complexity from $\mathcal{O}(K^L)$ to $\mathcal{O}(LK^{d+1})$ under bounded anchor assumptions. ATAT achieves state-of-the-art DLM perplexity across all 7 zero-shot benchmarks, outperforming even AR baselines on long-range tasks like LAMBADA.

The Uniform Masking Problem

Not all tokens are created equal — why treat them the same?

Content words like "quantum" and "probabilistic" carry 8–12 bits of surprisal, while function words like "the" and "of" carry only 2–4 bits. Existing DLMs mask all tokens with identical probability, wasting capacity on trivial predictions while underweighting informative ones.

⚠️ The frequency trap: ADLM uses token frequency as an importance proxy—but frequency is context-blind. The word "bank" has identical frequency whether anchoring a financial discussion or appearing peripherally in nature writing. ATAT solves this with learned hybrid importance.

Method

Three innovations that work together

Phase 1 — Core Innovation

Hybrid Importance Estimation

We combine two complementary signals into a unified importance score:

$$i^l = \lambda \cdot \underbrace{\sigma(\text{MLP}_\varphi(\mathbf{h}^l_{\text{GPT-2}}))}_{\text{contextual: locally surprising}} + (1-\lambda) \cdot \underbrace{\left(1 - \frac{\log(\text{freq}(x^l)+1)}{\log(\max_k \text{freq}(k)+1)}\right)}_{\text{frequency: globally rare}}$$

Why both? Frequency captures globally rare tokens that deserve attention across all contexts. Contextuality identifies locally surprising tokens whose importance depends on surrounding discourse. Common-but-important tokens (e.g., "The" in "The one who survived") are rescued by contextuality; rare-but-predictable domain terms are stabilized by frequency.

Phase 2 — Curriculum

Balanced Masking Schedule

Neither pure importance-proportional nor importance-inverse masking suffices. We introduce time-varying interpolation:

$$g_{\text{bal}}(i, t) = (1-t) \cdot g_{\text{inv}}(i) + t \cdot g_{\text{prop}}(i)$$

At $t \approx 1$ (heavy corruption): important tokens are preserved as semantic anchors — the model needs them for context.
At $t \approx 0$ (mostly recovered): training focuses on important tokens — allocating capacity where it matters most.

🎯 Intuition: Early in diffusion, keep the hard words visible so the model has context to predict easy words. Late in diffusion, test the model on hard words since it's already solved the easy ones.

Phase 3 — Training

Importance-Weighted Denoising

The full ATAT loss combines weighted denoising with importance supervision:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{ATAT}} = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{t,\mathbf{z}_t}\left[\sum_{l:z_t^l=[\text{M}]} (1+\beta \cdot i^l) \cdot \left(-\log p_\psi(x^l|\mathbf{z}_t, \mathbf{i})\right)\right]}_{\text{importance-weighted denoising}} + \gamma \underbrace{\frac{1}{L}\sum_l (i^l_{\text{learn}} - i^{l,*})^2}_{\text{importance supervision}}$$

Tokens with higher importance get higher weight $w(i) = 1 + \beta i$ in the loss, directing model capacity toward the predictions that matter most.

Theoretical Guarantees

Rigorous foundations for importance-guided diffusion

Theorem 1

Valid ELBO

The importance-weighted NELBO with $w(i) = 1 + \beta i$ provides a valid upper bound on negative log-likelihood:

$$-\log p_\theta(\mathbf{x}) \leq \frac{1}{\min_l w(i^l)} \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{IW}} + C$$
Proposition 1

Complexity Reduction

Under bounded anchor structure with $|\mathcal{A}| = d \ll L$ anchor tokens:

$$\mathcal{O}(K^L) \;\to\; \mathcal{O}(LK^{d+1})$$

Exponential → polynomial in sequence length

Proposition 2

Robustness Bound

With $\eta = 0.3$, the balanced masking is Lipschitz continuous w.r.t. importance error:

$$|g_{\text{bal}}(\hat{i}, t) - g_{\text{bal}}(i^*, t)| \leq 0.4\varepsilon_i$$

Errors in importance estimation are attenuated, not amplified

Contributions

1

Hybrid Importance Estimation

We develop a hybrid importance estimator combining learned contextual features (from frozen GPT-2) with frequency-based priors, achieving 0.82 correlation with oracle surprisal. The key finding is that neither signal alone suffices—their failure modes are complementary, yielding an 8.4% relative PPL reduction over uniform masking.

2

Balanced Masking Curriculum

We introduce a time-varying masking schedule that preserves important tokens early (as semantic anchors for context) and focuses on them late (for capacity allocation). This implements a principled token-level curriculum that adapts across diffusion timesteps—a fundamentally new approach to curriculum learning in diffusion models.

3

Theoretical Foundations

We prove importance-guided masking provides a valid ELBO bound, reduces sample complexity from $\mathcal{O}(K^L) \to \mathcal{O}(LK^{d+1})$, and is Lipschitz-robust to estimation error with constant 0.4—errors are attenuated, not amplified.

4

State-of-the-Art Efficiency

ATAT achieves SOTA zero-shot performance across all 7 benchmarks using 3.5× fewer trainable parameters (49M vs. 170M) and 10× fewer training tokens (6.5B vs. 65B) than ADLM—a 10× better PPL-per-compute ratio. It further generalizes to downstream DNA/protein tasks, demonstrating the universality of importance-aware masking.
