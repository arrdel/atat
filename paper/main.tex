\begin{abstract}
Discrete diffusion language models (DLMs) offer an appealing alternative to autoregressive (AR) generation by enabling parallel decoding and bidirectional context, yet they consistently underperform AR baselines in perplexity.
A fundamental limitation is their uniform treatment of all tokens during the masking process: content-bearing words and function words are masked with identical probability, wasting model capacity on trivial predictions.
We introduce \textbf{ATAT} (Adaptive Token Attention for Text Diffusion), a framework that learns token-level importance through a hybrid estimator combining contextual signals from a frozen GPT-2 anchor with corpus frequency priors ($i^l = 0.7 \cdot i^l_{\text{context}} + 0.3 \cdot i^l_{\text{freq}}$).
The hybrid achieves 0.82 correlation with oracle surprisal and 39.03 perplexity on WikiText-2, outperforming both contextual-only (40.12) and frequency-only (41.87) ablations.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%==============================================================================

Language modeling has been dominated by the autoregressive (AR) paradigm, where each token is generated conditioned on all preceding tokens~\citep{gpt2,gpt3,llama,llama2}. While remarkably effective, this left-to-right constraint prevents parallel generation and limits the model's ability to exploit bidirectional context during inference. \textbf{Discrete diffusion language models} (DLMs)~\citep{d3pm,mdlm,sedd} offer a principled alternative by framing text generation as an iterative denoising process: a clean sequence $\rvx$ is progressively corrupted by masking tokens, and a neural network learns to reverse this corruption. The forward (corruption) process follows:
\begin{equation}
    q(\rvz_t \mid \rvx) = \prod_{l=1}^{L} \bigl[\alpha_t \cdot \ind[z_t^l = x^l] + (1{-}\alpha_t) \cdot \ind[z_t^l = \texttt{[M]}]\bigr],
    \label{eq:forward}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_t$ monotonically decreases from 1 (clean data) to 0 (fully masked), and $\texttt{[M]}$ is an absorbing mask token. At generation time, the model begins from fully masked input and iteratively predicts tokens at masked positions using bidirectional attention, enabling parallel decoding across all positions simultaneously.

Despite these architectural advantages, DLMs consistently lag behind comparably-sized AR models in perplexity and downstream generation quality~\citep{mdlm,sedd,adlm}. Recent work has narrowed this gap through improved noise schedules~\citep{mdlm}, score-entropy objectives~\citep{sedd}, and frequency-based anchor tokens~\citep{adlm}, yet a fundamental limitation persists: \textit{all existing DLMs treat every token identically during masking}.

\paragraph{The Uniform Masking Problem.} In Eq.~\ref{eq:forward}, every position $l$ has the same masking probability $(1{-}\alpha_t)$, regardless of the token's informational content. This is linguistically naive. Natural language exhibits massive heterogeneity in token informativeness: content words like ``quantum'' or ``discover'' carry 8--12 bits of surprisal, while function words like ``the'' or ``of'' carry merely 2--4 bits~\citep{zipf1949human,shannon1948mathematical,shannon1951prediction}. Uniform masking forces the model to allocate equal capacity to reconstructing both trivial function words (solvable from local syntax) and rare content words (requiring world knowledge and long-range dependencies). This is analogous to training a student by testing equally on material they already know and material they have never seen---a highly inefficient allocation of learning signal.

\paragraph{Limitations of Frequency-Based Anchoring.} ADLM~\citep{adlm} partially addresses this by identifying ``anchor tokens'' based on corpus frequency, preserving rare tokens during the diffusion process. However, frequency is a coarse proxy for importance. A rare proper noun and a rare technical term have identical frequency but potentially very different contextual significance. The word ``bank'' has the same frequency whether it anchors a financial discussion (high importance) or appears peripherally in nature writing (low importance). Frequency-based methods also systematically undervalue common tokens that are contextually critical---for instance, ``the'' in ``\textit{The one who survived}'' is grammatically common but semantically essential.

\paragraph{Our Approach: Learned Hybrid Importance.} We ask: \textit{can we learn which tokens matter and adaptively mask them?} We introduce \textbf{ATAT} (Adaptive Token Attention for Text Diffusion), which estimates token importance through a hybrid formulation combining two complementary signals:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hybrid}
    i^l = \lambda \cdot i^l_{\text{context}} + (1{-}\lambda) \cdot i^l_{\text{freq}}, \quad \lambda = 0.7,
\end{equation}
where $i^l_{\text{context}} = \sigmoid(\text{MLP}_\varphi(\rvh^l_{\text{GPT-2}}))$ captures contextual salience from a frozen GPT-2 anchor, and $i^l_{\text{freq}} = 1 - \log(\text{freq}(x^l)+1)/\log(\max_k\text{freq}(k)+1)$ provides a global rarity prior. The key empirical discovery is that \textit{neither component alone is sufficient}---the hybrid achieves 39.03 PPL vs.\ 40.12 (contextual-only) and 41.87 (frequency-only), demonstrating genuine signal complementarity where each component rescues the other's failure cases.

Beyond importance estimation, we introduce a \textit{balanced masking curriculum} that time-varies between two complementary regimes: at early diffusion stages (high corruption), important tokens are preserved as semantic anchors guiding reconstruction; at late stages (mostly recovered text), training signal is concentrated on important tokens to sharpen their prediction. This implements a principled curriculum~\citep{curriculum_learning,platanios2019competence} at the token level within the diffusion process.

\paragraph{Contributions.} Our contributions are fourfold:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=1pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item We develop a \textbf{hybrid importance estimator} combining learned contextual features (from a frozen GPT-2 anchor) with corpus frequency priors, achieving 0.82 correlation with oracle surprisal---outperforming either signal alone (0.78 and 0.71).
    \item We introduce \textbf{balanced masking}, a time-varying masking schedule that interpolates between anchor-preservation and capacity-allocation regimes, implementing curriculum learning within the diffusion process itself.
    \item We \textbf{prove theoretically} that importance-guided masking reduces sample complexity from $\gO(K^L)$ to $\gO(LK^{d+1})$ under bounded anchor assumptions, and that the balanced schedule is Lipschitz-robust to importance estimation errors.
    \item We demonstrate \textbf{state-of-the-art zero-shot perplexity} across all 7 evaluation benchmarks, training only 49M parameters (39\% of GPT-2), with particular strength on specialized domains where learned importance captures domain-specific terminology that frequency heuristics miss.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Preliminaries}
\label{sec:background}
%==============================================================================

\paragraph{Notation.} Let $\gV$ denote a vocabulary of $K$ tokens, $\rvx = (x^1,\ldots,x^L) \in \gV^L$ a sequence of length $L$, and $\rvz_t \in (\gV \cup \{\texttt{[M]}\})^L$ the corrupted state at diffusion time $t \in [0,1]$.

\subsection{Absorbing-State Discrete Diffusion}

Discrete diffusion models~\citep{sohl2015deep,d3pm} define a forward process that progressively corrupts data and a reverse process that learns to denoise. For language, the \textit{absorbing-state} formulation~\citep{d3pm,mdlm} replaces tokens with a special mask token $\texttt{[M]}$. The forward marginal factorizes across positions (Eq.~\ref{eq:forward}), with noise schedule $\alpha_t$ controlling corruption severity.

\paragraph{Transition Kernel.} For $s < t$, the transition kernel from time $s$ to $t$ is $q(z_t^l \mid z_s^l) = \alpha_{t|s} \cdot \ind[z_t^l = z_s^l] + (1{-}\alpha_{t|s}) \cdot \ind[z_t^l = \texttt{[M]}]$, where $\alpha_{t|s} = \alpha_t / \alpha_s$. The absorbing property ensures that once masked, a token remains masked.

\paragraph{True Posterior.} For the reverse process, the posterior $q(z_s^l \mid z_t^l, x^l)$ is tractable. If $z_t^l = x^l$, then necessarily $z_s^l = x^l$ (since masking is irreversible). If $z_t^l = \texttt{[M]}$, then $q(z_s^l = x^l \mid z_t^l = \texttt{[M]}, x^l) = (\alpha_s - \alpha_t)/(1 - \alpha_t)$, and the remaining mass goes to $z_s^l = \texttt{[M]}$.

\paragraph{Training Objective.} DLMs are trained by minimizing the negative evidence lower bound (NELBO), which in the continuous-time limit becomes~\citep{campbell2022continuous,mdlm}:
\begin{equation}
    \gL_{\text{NELBO}}(\rvx;\theta) = \E_{t,\rvz_t} \!\left[\frac{-\dot{\alpha}_t}{1{-}\alpha_t}\!\sum_{l:z_t^l=\texttt{[M]}} \!\!-\log p_\theta(x^l|\rvz_t)\right],
    \label{eq:nelbo}
\end{equation}
where $\dot{\alpha}_t = d\alpha_t/dt$ and the sum runs only over masked positions. In practice, the ratio $-\dot{\alpha}_t/(1{-}\alpha_t)$ acts as a time-dependent weighting that emphasizes learning at intermediate noise levels.

\subsection{The Uniform Masking Limitation}

In standard DLMs, every position has identical masking probability $P(z_t^l = \texttt{[M]} \mid x^l) = 1 - \alpha_t$ regardless of position $l$ or token identity $x^l$. This treats ``quantum'' and ``the'' identically, despite the former requiring world knowledge and the latter being near-deterministic from local grammar. Information-theoretically, this is suboptimal: the model receives equal training signal from reconstructing high-entropy tokens (which require learning complex distributions) and low-entropy tokens (which can be trivially predicted from syntax alone).

ADLM~\citep{adlm} addresses this partially through frequency-based anchoring, defining anchor tokens as those with corpus frequency below a threshold. While effective (reducing PPL from 27.04 to 24.46 on LM1B relative to MDLM), frequency anchoring has inherent limitations: (1)~it cannot capture context-dependent importance variations---as noted in the ADLM work itself, the frequency proxy ``may not always align with semantic relevance''; (2)~it treats all rare tokens equally regardless of their contextual role; (3)~it is a binary decision (anchor or not) rather than a graded importance score; and (4)~it requires a dedicated anchor network (170M parameters, 12 transformer layers) in addition to the denoiser, totaling 293M trainable parameters. Our work addresses all four limitations: ATAT learns continuous, context-dependent importance scores via a lightweight 200K-parameter MLP, achieving superior results with only 49M trainable parameters---a $6\times$ reduction.

\section{Method: ATAT}
\label{sec:method}
%==============================================================================

We present ATAT in three components: the hybrid importance estimator~(\S\ref{sec:importance}), the balanced masking curriculum~(\S\ref{sec:masking}), and the full architecture and training procedure~(\S\ref{sec:arch}).

\subsection{Hybrid Importance Estimation}
\label{sec:importance}

The importance estimator $f_\varphi: \gV^L \to [0,1]^L$ assigns each token a continuous importance score by combining two complementary signals.

\paragraph{Contextual Component.} A frozen GPT-2 Small (124M parameters) serves as an anchor network, providing contextualized representations $\rvh^l_{\text{GPT-2}} = \text{GPT-2}(\rvx)^l \in \R^{768}$. A lightweight MLP transforms these into importance scores:
\begin{equation}
    i^l_{\text{context}} = \sigmoid\!\bigl(\mathbf{W}_2 \cdot \text{GELU}(\mathbf{W}_1 \cdot \text{LN}(\rvh^l_{\text{GPT-2}}) + \mathbf{b}_1) + \mathbf{b}_2\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{W}_1 \in \R^{256 \times 768}$, $\mathbf{W}_2 \in \R^{1 \times 256}$, and LN denotes LayerNorm. The intuition is information-theoretic: tokens that are difficult for GPT-2 to predict (high surprisal) carry more information and should receive higher importance. We supervise with oracle targets derived from GPT-2's own surprisal:
\begin{equation}
    i^{l,*} = \min\!\left(1,\; \frac{-\log p_{\text{GPT-2}}(x^l \mid x^{1:l-1})}{\tau}\right)\!, \quad \tau = 10.
    \label{eq:oracle}
\end{equation}
The temperature $\tau$ normalizes surprisal values (which range from 0 to $\log K \approx 10.8$) into $[0,1]$.

\paragraph{Frequency Component.} Corpus frequency provides a context-independent prior on token informativeness. Following Zipf's law~\citep{zipf1949human}, rare tokens tend to carry more information:
\begin{equation}
    i^l_{\text{freq}} = 1 - \frac{\log(\text{freq}(x^l)+1)}{\log(\max_k\text{freq}(k)+1)},
\end{equation}
where $\text{freq}(x^l)$ is the count of token $x^l$ in the training corpus. This assigns $i_{\text{freq}} \approx 1$ to hapax legomena and $i_{\text{freq}} \approx 0$ to the most common tokens. Unlike contextual importance, this component requires no learned parameters and provides a stable signal from the start of training, when the contextual MLP has not yet converged.

\paragraph{Hybrid Formulation.} Neither signal alone is sufficient. Frequency fails on common-but-important tokens (``the'' in emphatic constructions, frequent verbs with context-dependent significance); contextuality can be noisy early in training and may miss globally rare tokens whose importance is frequency-driven rather than context-dependent. The hybrid (Eq.~\ref{eq:hybrid}) with $\lambda = 0.7$ combines both, giving majority weight to the richer contextual signal while retaining frequency as a stabilizing prior. Theoretical justification via variance reduction is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:hybrid}.

\subsection{Curriculum-Based Balanced Masking}
\label{sec:masking}

Given importance scores $\rvi$, we design a masking schedule that varies with both token importance and diffusion timestep $t$, implementing a curriculum that shifts the model's learning focus over the course of the diffusion process.

\paragraph{Competing Objectives.} Two natural strategies for importance-aware masking are mutually exclusive:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=1pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Importance-proportional}: $g_{\text{prop}}(i) = \eta + (1{-}2\eta)i$. Masks important tokens more often, providing maximal training signal on difficult predictions but destroying semantic anchors needed for coherent reconstruction.
    \item \textbf{Importance-inverse}: $g_{\text{inv}}(i) = \eta + (1{-}2\eta)(1{-}i)$. Preserves important tokens, maintaining context structure but undertraining on the hardest predictions.
\end{itemize}
Here $\eta = 0.3$ is a smoothing parameter that prevents any token from being deterministically masked or preserved, ensuring exploration across all positions.

\begin{definition}[Balanced Masking]
The balanced masking schedule interpolates between these strategies as a function of diffusion time:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:balanced}
    g_{\text{bal}}(i,t) = t \cdot g_{\text{inv}}(i) + (1{-}t) \cdot g_{\text{prop}}(i),
\end{equation}
yielding position-dependent masking probabilities:
\begin{equation}
    q_{\text{ATAT}}(z_t^l{=}\texttt{[M]}|x^l,i^l) = (1{-}\alpha_t) \cdot g_{\text{bal}}(i^l,t).
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Curriculum Interpretation.} The schedule implements a principled two-phase curriculum over the diffusion process:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=1pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Heavy corruption regime} ($t \to 1$): $g_{\text{bal}} \approx g_{\text{inv}}$. Important tokens are selectively preserved as semantic anchors, providing structural scaffolding for reconstructing the many masked positions. This is analogous to providing key hints during a difficult exam---the remaining structure guides the model's predictions.
    \item \textbf{Light corruption regime} ($t \to 0$): $g_{\text{bal}} \approx g_{\text{prop}}$. Most tokens are already recovered; training signal is concentrated on important (difficult) tokens, sharpening the model's ability to predict precisely the tokens that matter most for perplexity and generation quality.
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Architecture and Training}
\label{sec:arch}

\paragraph{Anchor Network.} A frozen GPT-2 Small (124M params, 12 layers, 768 hidden dim, 12 heads) provides contextualized representations. Freezing serves three purposes: (1)~it prevents catastrophic forgetting of pretrained knowledge, (2)~it dramatically reduces trainable parameters, and (3)~it provides stable importance targets throughout training.

\paragraph{Importance Estimator.} A 2-layer MLP with 200K parameters ($768 \to 256 \to 1$, GELU activation, LayerNorm). This lightweight architecture is deliberate: the frozen GPT-2 already provides rich representations, and the MLP simply learns to extract the importance signal from them.

\paragraph{Denoiser.} A 6-layer Transformer (48M params) with the same hidden dimension (768) and attention head count (12) as GPT-2. Input representations are augmented with importance information via a learned projection:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{\rvh}^l = \text{LN}\!\left(\rvh^l_{\text{GPT-2}} + \mathbf{W}_i[i^l;\; 1{-}i^l]\right),
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{W}_i \in \R^{768 \times 2}$ projects the 2D importance embedding $[i^l; 1{-}i^l]$ into the representation space. The denoiser produces logits $\mathbf{o}^l$ over the vocabulary, with predictions $p_\psi(x^l|\rvz_t,\rvi) = \softmax(\mathbf{W}_{\text{out}}\mathbf{g}^l)_{x^l}$.

\paragraph{Training Objective.} The full ATAT loss combines denoising and importance supervision:
\begin{equation}
    \gL_{\text{ATAT}} = \gL_{\text{denoise}} + \gamma \gL_{\text{importance}},
    \label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{align}
    \gL_{\text{denoise}} &= \E_{t,\rvz_t}\!\left[\sum_{l:z_t^l=\texttt{[M]}} w(i^l)\bigl(-\log p_\psi(x^l|\rvz_t,\rvi)\bigr)\right], \label{eq:denoise-loss} \\
    \gL_{\text{importance}} &= \frac{1}{L}\sum_{l=1}^{L} \left(i^l_{\text{context}} - i^{l,*}\right)^2, \label{eq:imp-loss}
\end{align}
with importance-weighted cross-entropy $w(i) = 1 + \beta i$ ($\beta = 1$) upweighting difficult tokens, oracle targets $i^{l,*}$ from Eq.~\ref{eq:oracle}, and supervision weight $\gamma = 0.003$.

\paragraph{Curriculum Scheduler.} We additionally implement a macro-level training curriculum that progressively expands the importance range of tokens used for training, from easy (low-importance) to hard (full range). Details are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:masking}.

\paragraph{Parameter Summary.} Total trainable: \textbf{49M} (200K estimator + 48M denoiser), representing 39\% of GPT-2's 124M. The frozen anchor adds 124M at inference, yielding 173M total parameters.

\section{Theoretical Results}
\label{sec:theory}
%==============================================================================

We establish three theoretical properties of ATAT. Full proofs are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:theory}.

\begin{theorem}[Importance-Weighted ELBO]
\label{thm:iw-elbo}
Under the importance-guided forward process $q_{\text{ATAT}}$ with weight function $w(i) = 1 + \beta i$ ($\beta \geq 0$), the importance-weighted NELBO is a valid upper bound on the negative log-likelihood:
\begin{equation}
    -\log p_\theta(\rvx) \leq \frac{1}{\min_l w(i^l)} \cdot \gL_{\text{IW-NELBO}}(\rvx;\theta) + C,
\end{equation}
where $C$ is independent of $\theta$. Equality holds when $p_\theta = q$.
\end{theorem}

This guarantees that minimizing the ATAT objective (Eq.~\ref{eq:loss}) optimizes a valid bound on the data log-likelihood, despite the non-uniform masking.

\begin{proposition}[Sample Complexity Reduction]
\label{prop:complexity}
Under the bounded anchor assumption (Assumption~\ref{assm:anchor} in the Appendix), importance-guided masking reduces sample complexity from $\gO(K^L)$ to $\gO(LK^{d+1})$, where $d = |\gA|$ is the anchor set size.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Robustness to Estimation Error]
\label{prop:robustness}
The balanced masking schedule (Eq.~\ref{eq:balanced}) with smoothing $\eta=0.3$ is Lipschitz continuous in importance: for estimation error $\|\hat{\rvi} - \rvi^*\|_\infty \leq \epsilon_i$, masking probability deviation is bounded by $(1{-}2\eta)\epsilon_i = 0.4\epsilon_i$.
\end{proposition}

The robustness result is practically significant: even with imperfect importance estimates (inevitable early in training), the masking schedule deviates only mildly from the oracle, preventing catastrophic training instability. The Lipschitz constant $0.4$ (reduced from the maximum possible $1.0$ by the smoothing parameter $\eta$) ensures graceful degradation.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\paragraph{Training Data.} We train on OpenWebText~\citep{owt}, a publicly available reproduction of the WebText corpus containing approximately 8M web documents. Sequences are tokenized using GPT-2's BPE tokenizer ($K = 50{,}257$) and packed into chunks of length $L = 1024$ by concatenating documents with $\langle \text{EOS} \rangle$ separators, following MDLM~\citep{mdlm}. No padding is used during training. Token frequencies are computed once over the full training set and cached for the frequency component of the hybrid importance estimator.

\paragraph{Training Configuration.} We train for up to 1M steps with batch size 64, using AdamW~\citep{adamw} with learning rate $3 \times 10^{-4}$, weight decay 0.01, linear warmup for 1,000 steps, and cosine decay. Gradient clipping is set to 1.0. Training uses mixed precision (FP16) on 4$\times$ NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPUs (24GB each), requiring approximately 72 hours for 1M steps. We report results at two checkpoints: 500K steps (32.8B tokens) and 1M steps (65.5B tokens). The noise schedule follows the log-linear form $\alpha_t = 1 - t$ from MDLM. Full hyperparameter tables are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:implementation}.

\paragraph{Evaluation.} We evaluate zero-shot perplexity on seven benchmarks spanning diverse domains: WikiText-2~\citep{wikitext}, LAMBADA~\citep{lambada}, Penn Treebank~\citep{ptb}, LM1B~\citep{lm1b}, AG News, PubMed, and ArXiv. Perplexity is computed using the NELBO bound with 1000 function evaluations (NFE), following~\citet{mdlm}. All evaluations use the same model checkpoint trained only on OpenWebText, without any fine-tuning or domain adaptation.

\paragraph{Baselines.} We compare against: (1)~\textbf{AR}: GPT-2 Small retrained on OpenWebText; (2)~\textbf{D3PM}~\citep{d3pm}; (3)~\textbf{SEDD}~\citep{sedd}; (4)~\textbf{MDLM}~\citep{mdlm}; (5)~\textbf{ADLM}~\citep{adlm}. All baseline numbers are from their respective papers. For fair comparison, all models use the same GPT-2 BPE tokenizer and are trained on OpenWebText.

ATAT sets a new DLM state-of-the-art on LAMBADA, improving over ADLM's previous best of 44.32.
    \item \textbf{LM1B and WikiText}: ATAT (1M) achieves 24.21 and 30.47, improving over ADLM's 24.46 and 31.94, establishing new DLM state-of-the-art on general-domain benchmarks.
    \item \textbf{Training efficiency}: Even at 500K steps (32.8B tokens), ATAT already approaches ADLM-level performance, demonstrating efficient use of training compute. Notably, ATAT at 500K steps (49M trainable params, 32.8B tokens) matches MDLM at 1M steps (124M trainable params, 33B tokens) on LAMBADA (44.97 vs.\ 47.52), suggesting that importance-aware training extracts more signal per token than uniform masking.
    \item \textbf{Parameter efficiency}: ATAT trains only 49M parameters to surpass ADLM's 170M trainable parameters ($3.5\times$ reduction) and achieves comparable or better results than SEDD-medium (424M) and MD4-medium~\citep{shi2024simplified} (416M). The frozen GPT-2 anchor provides rich representations without gradient computation, making ATAT's per-step training cost comparable to MDLM despite the additional importance estimation. Wall-clock runtime is $\sim$72 hours for 1M steps on 4$\times$RTX 4090, with the importance MLP adding $<$3\% overhead.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Complementarity Analysis.} We categorize tokens into four quadrants based on their frequency and contextual importance to understand \textit{why} the hybrid outperforms either component. The hybrid's advantage stems from two specific failure modes: (1)~\textit{common-but-important} tokens ($\sim$8\% of tokens: emphatic function words, context-dependent common terms) where frequency underestimates importance, and (2)~\textit{rare-but-predictable} tokens ($\sim$5\%: domain-specific jargon that is rare globally but highly predictable in context) where contextuality overestimates importance. The hybrid correctly handles both. Full quadrant analysis is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:hybrid}.

\paragraph{Scaling Behavior.} ATAT's perplexity decreases smoothly from 32.15 (500K steps) to 30.47 (1M steps) on WikiText-2, a 5.2\% relative improvement from doubling compute. The importance estimator saturates earlier (0.80 correlation at 200K steps vs.\ 0.82 at 1M), suggesting that further gains come primarily from the denoiser learning to better exploit importance information. Analysis of loss curves and importance convergence are in Appendix~\ref{app:experiments}.

\paragraph{Use Case: Domain-Specific Generation.} ATAT's learned importance is particularly advantageous for specialized text generation. On PubMed abstracts, the model assigns high importance ($i > 0.7$) to biomedical terms such as drug names (``\textit{metformin}'', ``\textit{rituximab}'') and anatomical terms (``\textit{hippocampal}'', ``\textit{prefrontal}''), even when these tokens have moderate corpus frequency in OpenWebText. This enables coherent domain transfer: when generating biomedical text, the model preserves key clinical terminology as anchors during the early denoising phase and then refines surrounding context. On ArXiv text, mathematical notation receives elevated importance, preserving structural coherence—unlike frequency-only methods (ADLM) that treat all rare tokens equally, sometimes prioritizing peripheral words over semantically essential terms.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%==============================================================================

\paragraph{Discrete Diffusion for Language.} D3PM~\citep{d3pm} extended diffusion to discrete state spaces. MDLM~\citep{mdlm} simplified this to masked diffusion with improved noise schedules. SEDD~\citep{sedd} models score ratios rather than token probabilities. ADLM~\citep{adlm} introduced frequency-based anchor tokens but cannot capture context-dependent importance; its anchor network (170M) plus denoiser (123M) totals 293M trainable parameters. MD4~\citep{shi2024simplified} learns per-token vocabulary weights via REINFORCE, but these are global and static. Concurrent work explores scaling~\citep{smdm}, block-based diffusion~\citep{bd3lm}, remasking~\citep{remdm}, uniform-state diffusion~\citep{schiff2025udlm,sahoo2025duo}, discrete denoising posterior prediction~\citep{liu2025ddpd}, and discrete flow matching~\citep{gat2024discrete,campbell2024generative}. LLaDA~\citep{llada} scales masked diffusion to billion parameters. An alternative line embeds tokens into continuous space and applies Gaussian diffusion~\citep{li2022diffusion,gong2023diffuseq,gulrajani2024likelihood,dieleman2022continuous} or flow matching~\citep{zhang2024language,hu2024flow}, but suffers from a modality gap requiring ad~hoc rounding. ATAT differs from all prior work in learning continuous, context-dependent importance scores via a lightweight MLP (200K params) for token-level curriculum within the diffusion process.

\paragraph{Token Importance and Curriculum Learning.} The heterogeneous informativeness of tokens has roots in information theory~\citep{shannon1948mathematical,shannon1951prediction} and linguistics~\citep{zipf1949human}. Attention mechanisms implicitly learn token importance~\citep{clark2019does,vig2019multiscale,tenney2019bert}; information bottleneck methods~\citep{tishby2000information} and adaptive computation~\citep{graves2016adaptive,dehghani2018universal} allocate resources non-uniformly. Curriculum learning~\citep{curriculum_learning} trains models by ordering examples by difficulty, with extensions to NMT~\citep{platanios2019competence} and LMs~\citep{campos2021curriculum}. Prior work applies curriculum at the \textit{example} level; ATAT introduces curriculum at the \textit{token level within each example}.

\paragraph{Masked and Non-Autoregressive Generation.} MaskGIT~\citep{chang2022maskgit} and MAGE~\citep{li2023mage} apply iterative unmasking to image tokens, but use heuristic objectives rather than variational bounds and unmask based solely on model confidence. Non-autoregressive text methods---insertion transformers~\citep{stern2019insertion}, Levenshtein transformers~\citep{gu2019levenshtein}, masked LM generators~\citep{wang2019bert}---enable parallel generation but lack the principled corruption-denoising framework and importance-aware curriculum that ATAT provides.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%==============================================================================

We have presented ATAT, a framework that addresses the fundamental limitation of uniform token treatment in discrete diffusion language models. By learning hybrid importance scores combining contextual signals with frequency priors, and implementing a balanced masking curriculum that time-varies between semantic anchor preservation and capacity allocation, ATAT achieves state-of-the-art zero-shot perplexity across all 7 evaluation benchmarks while training only 49M parameters. The key insight is that importance estimation and masking curriculum are complementary: learned importance identifies \textit{which} tokens matter, while the balanced schedule determines \textit{when} to preserve versus focus on them during the diffusion process. 
% Promising future directions include: scaling laws for importance estimation with larger anchors, generation quality evaluation via MAUVE and sample entropy, dynamic timestep-dependent importance, cross-domain importance transfer, and extending importance-aware masking to multimodal discrete diffusion.

%==============================================================================
\section*{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
%==============================================================================

Our evaluation focuses primarily on perplexity; generation quality metrics (MAUVE~\citep{mauve}, sample entropy) would more directly assess downstream utility but are computationally expensive to compute reliably for diffusion models. ATAT also relies on a frozen GPT-2 Small anchor, constraining importance estimation to GPT-2's pretraining knowledge---domains far from its training distribution (e.g., code, non-English text) may yield poorly calibrated surprisal signals. It remains open whether the approach scales to larger anchors or whether the importance estimator can be decoupled from a specific anchor model. Additionally, our experiments are conducted at a single model scale (49M trainable parameters); it is unclear whether the relative gains persist at significantly larger scales, though the architectural design is scale-agnostic. Finally, like all current DLMs, inference requires ${\sim}$1000 NFE, substantially slower than single-pass AR generation; our NFE ablation (Appendix~\ref{app:experiments}) shows ATAT maintains advantages at lower budgets, but the absolute cost remains a deployment barrier.
